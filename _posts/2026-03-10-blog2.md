### ***Exploring User Research: Understanding People, Needs & Assumptions***

#### **My Research Process**
For my project, I investigated the problem of how overwhelming and fragmented travel planning can be. Many travelers have to switch between multiple websites to compare flight prices, hotel costs, and plan itineraries, which makes the process stressful and time-consuming. Initially, I assumed that the main frustration users experienced was simply price comparison—that people just wanted a faster way to find cheaper trips. I believed that displaying a combined cost upfront would immediately solve most of their planning difficulties.

To better understand user needs, I conducted surveys and semi-structured interviews with friends and classmates who travel at least once a year. The survey helped me gather general patterns about how people currently book trips, what tools they use, and their biggest frustrations. It also allowed me reach out to people who might not live in the same time zone, or has a tight schedule and is unavailable for a chat. The interviews allowed me to explore deeper questions, such as how they feel during the planning process and what typically causes stress or confusion. 

After collecting the data, I analyzed responses by identifying recurring themes and grouping them into categories like price sensitivity, information overload, difficulty coordinating dates, and lack of itinerary organization. I created simple affinity groupings to spot patterns and noticed that while cost was important, users were equally frustrated by switching between multiple platforms and keeping track of bookings. This helped me realize that my app should not only focus on combined pricing, but also on creating a streamlined and organized planning experience. The research shifted my perspective from just “finding the cheapest trip” to “reducing cognitive overload during travel planning.”

#### **What You Learned**
What surprised me most during this process was realizing that price is not the only — or even the biggest — source of stress in travel planning. I initially assumed that users mainly struggled with finding the cheapest combination of flights and hotels, and that showing a clear combined price would solve most of their problems. However, my research revealed that many travelers feel overwhelmed by the number of decisions involved, from comparing dates to coordinating bookings and organizing activities. This shifted my assumption from focusing purely on cost to understanding that decision fatigue and information overload are major pain points.

![Survey](/assets/images/survey.jpg)

One of the most valuable insights I gathered was that users want clarity and confidence, not just lower prices. They want to feel organized and in control throughout the planning process. As a result, my project evolved from being just a price comparison tool to becoming a more comprehensive travel planning system. I began prioritizing features that simplify choices, visually organize trip details, and integrate itinerary planning to reduce stress and make the experience feel smoother overall.

#### **Applying These Learnings to Life & Career**
One of the most valuable aspects of user research in everyday life is learning how to truly listen. User research teaches you to ask open-ended questions, avoid assumptions, and understand situations from another person’s perspective. These skills are useful not just in design, but in friendships, teamwork, and conflict resolution. Instead of jumping to solutions, I’ve learned the importance of understanding the root of a problem first.

In my future career, whether in tech, business, or leadership, understanding users will be essential. Every product, service, or decision ultimately affects people. Being able to recognize patterns in behavior, identify pain points, and design solutions around real needs can lead to more thoughtful and successful outcomes. In leadership especially, empathy and active listening help build trust and create systems that genuinely support others rather than just meeting surface-level goals.

This experience also changed how I view technology. I now see that technology is not just functional—it shapes emotions, behaviors, and decision-making. A poorly designed system can create stress and confusion, while a thoughtful one can build confidence and clarity. Personally, conducting research taught me humility. I realized that my assumptions are often incomplete, and meaningful solutions require curiosity, patience, and empathy. User research is ultimately about understanding people, and that mindset extends far beyond this project.
