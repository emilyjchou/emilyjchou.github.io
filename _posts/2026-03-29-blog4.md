### HCI Blog Series (4/6): From Idea to Reality — My Rapid Prototyping Experience

#### The First Attempt: Bringing My Idea to Life

After finishing my concept video for TripFlow, I was excited to finally bring the idea into something tangible. But honestly, my first prototype was… pretty rough.

I started with a very simple layout — just a few screens showing:
- A page to input travel dates  
- A basic results page showing cheaper options  
- A simple dashboard with trip details  

At this stage, it didn’t look like a real app at all. It was more like a rough sketch of what the app *could* be. Some buttons didn’t go anywhere, and the flow wasn’t completely clear. But that was kind of the point — I wasn’t trying to make it perfect, just something testable.

When deciding what features to include, I focused only on the core ideas:
1. Finding the cheapest travel dates  
2. Simplifying trip planning  
3. Keeping all trip information in one place  

I avoided adding too many features because I didn’t want to overcomplicate the prototype early on.

To build it, I used a digital wireframing tool (like Figma), which made it easy to quickly design screens and connect them into a basic interactive flow. I also sketched a few ideas on paper first, just to figure out the layout before jumping into the digital version.

---

#### Testing & Learning: Gathering Early Feedback

Once I had a working prototype, I asked a few friends and classmates to try it out. Most of them were students, which made them a perfect fit for my target users.

Watching people use my prototype was honestly one of the most eye-opening parts of this process.

Some things worked well:
- Users immediately understood the idea of finding cheaper dates  
- The overall concept made sense to them  
- They liked the idea of having everything in one place  

But there were also issues I didn’t expect.

Some users were confused about:
- Where to click first  
- What certain buttons were supposed to do  
- How the “flexible dates” feature actually worked  

One surprising moment was when someone said:
> “I like the idea, but I don’t really know what I’m supposed to do first.”

That hit me hard because I thought the flow was obvious — but clearly, it wasn’t.

Another user mentioned:
> “It feels useful, but I’d want it to guide me more.”

That made me realize that just having features isn’t enough — the experience has to feel intuitive.

---

#### Iteration & Improvement: Refining My Prototype

Based on the feedback, I made several important changes.

First, I improved the navigation by:
- Adding clearer buttons  
- Making the starting point more obvious  
- Simplifying the layout  

Then, I focused on clarity:
- I added short descriptions to explain features  
- Made labels more user-friendly  
- Reduced unnecessary elements that caused confusion  

I also improved the flow so that users could move through the app more naturally, instead of feeling stuck or unsure.

When deciding what to change, I prioritized:
1. Issues that confused users  
2. Anything that slowed down interaction  
3. Features that weren’t clearly understood  

After these changes, the prototype felt much smoother. It still wasn’t perfect, but it was noticeably easier to use.

This process showed me how quickly a design can improve just by listening to users.

---

#### Personal Reflection: The Rapid Prototyping Experience

The most challenging part of rapid prototyping for me was accepting that my first version wouldn’t be good.

As someone who likes things to be polished, it felt uncomfortable to show something incomplete. But I realized that waiting for perfection would have slowed everything down.

What I enjoyed most was seeing how real users interacted with my idea. It was interesting to watch where they hesitated, what they understood instantly, and what confused them. It made the design process feel much more real.

If I had to give advice to someone doing rapid prototyping for the first time, I would say:

- Don’t try to make it perfect — just make it testable  
- Get feedback as early as possible  
- Pay attention to how users behave, not just what they say  
- Be open to changing your ideas  

This experience taught me that good design doesn’t come from guessing — it comes from testing, learning, and improving.

Rapid prototyping isn’t about getting it right the first time. It’s about getting closer each time.

And for me, this was the moment where my idea truly started becoming something real.
